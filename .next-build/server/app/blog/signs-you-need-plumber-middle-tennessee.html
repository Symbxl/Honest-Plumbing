7 Signs You Need to Call a Middle Tennessee Plumber (Don't Wait)

Some plumbing issues resolve themselves. These seven don't — and waiting usually makes the eventual fix bigger, slower, and more expensive.

AK

Aaron Kelly

Licensed Master Plumber, Honest Plumbing

1. Hot water that runs hot but slow

If you have good hot water pressure on day one of moving in, and over the years the hot has gotten noticeably weaker than the cold, you almost certainly have galvanized supply line corrosion. Galvanized steel was the standard supply pipe in Middle Tennessee homes built before about 1960, and it corrodes from the inside out, with the corrosion accelerating on the hot-water side because heat speeds the reaction. By the time the difference is obvious, the lines are 60–80% closed internally. This is one of the most common patterns we see in Murfreesboro's Lockeland Springs, the older streets of La Vergne, and pre-war Brentwood homes. The fix is partial or full re-pipe (typically PEX or copper), and it should be planned, not deferred to when something bursts.

2. Sewer smell in or around the house

A sewer smell that comes and goes — especially after heavy rain, or after running a lot of water through the house — usually means one of three things: a dry p-trap at an under-used floor drain or guest bathroom, a cracked or compromised sewer vent stack, or a partial blockage in the main sewer lateral causing sewer gas to back up through the line. None of these are emergencies, but all three should be diagnosed promptly because the underlying cause sometimes worsens over weeks or months. If the smell is strong, constant, and accompanied by slow drains anywhere in the house, you likely have a main line issue — get a camera inspection done.

When sewer smell is actually a methane or gas safety issue

If the smell is gas (sulfur-like, rotten-egg) rather than sewer (musty, septic), get out of the building, call Piedmont Natural Gas at 1-800-752-7504, and don't operate any electrical switches on your way out. Methane buildup in a basement or crawl space is a life-safety problem, not a plumbing inconvenience. Once Piedmont has located the leak and shut off service, that's when you call us to repair and coordinate restoration with Metro Codes.

3. Water bill that doubled with no usage change

A water bill that suddenly doubles or triples without a corresponding usage change is almost always an underground leak — typically in the water service line from the meter to the house, or in a slab leak under the foundation. Metro Water Services in Middle Tennessee bills based on metered usage, so the meter doesn't lie. Run this check: turn off every fixture in the house, then look at the meter dial. If the small triangular leak indicator is moving, water is flowing somewhere it shouldn't. Service line leaks are a $2,800–$7,500 repair depending on length and location, but the longer you ignore them the more your water bill compounds — and a major underground leak can undermine foundation work if it runs long enough.

4. Recurring main line back-ups (more than once in 12 months)

A single main sewer line back-up is unfortunate but normal — most homes with mature trees will have one every 5–10 years from accumulated root intrusion. Two or more back-ups in 12 months means the line has a structural defect (a crack, a sag/belly, or a collapse), and cabling will only buy you a few months at a time. The right move at this point is a sewer camera inspection ($295 in the Middle Tennessee, waived if it leads to a repair we perform), which gives you a clear video record of what's actually happening underground and lets you make an informed decision about repair vs. replacement.

5. Water heater that's making rumbling, popping, or knocking sounds

Tank water heaters develop sediment buildup at the bottom of the tank over time (calcium and magnesium deposits, accelerated in Middle Tennessee's moderately hard water). When the burner fires, the sediment layer creates pockets of trapped steam that release with a rumble, pop, or knock — the sound is steam, not pipe issues. Early-stage sediment can be flushed out (one of the reasons we recommend annual maintenance), but past about 8–10 years of accumulated buildup, the tank is at the end of its useful life. If your water heater is making these sounds and is more than 8 years old, plan for replacement before it fails at the worst possible moment.

6. Discolored water — brown, rust-tinted, or cloudy

Brown or rust-colored water that comes out hot or appears intermittently is usually iron oxide (rust) from corroding galvanized supply lines or from a corroded water heater tank — common in older Middle Tennessee homes. Cloudy water that clears from the bottom up is usually just air in the line (not a problem). Brown water that comes out cold and persistently is more concerning — it can indicate a problem with the service line from Metro Water, in which case you should call Metro Water Services first (615-862-4600) before assuming it's your plumbing. We do free water-quality consultations when patterns aren't obvious.

7. Frozen or burst pipes after a hard freeze

Middle Tennessee gets hard freezes most winters, and even newer Brentwood and Franklin homes are vulnerable when temperatures drop into the single digits with sustained wind. If you've had a freeze event and have any of: no water at one or more fixtures, water in the basement or crawl space, audible water running with no fixture on, or visible bulging or icing on exposed pipe — shut your main water supply off immediately at the meter shutoff, and call us. Don't try to thaw with a propane torch (we see this every freeze and it's how house fires start). We have emergency response for active leaks 24/7.

When to call a Middle Tennessee plumber vs. when to wait

Emergency same-day call: active leaks, no water in the house, no hot water, sewer back-up that's affecting living spaces, suspected gas leak (after Piedmont). Schedule within a week: slow drains across multiple fixtures, recurring single-fixture clogs, water heater making sounds or showing rust, water bill anomalies, sewer smell. Schedule when convenient: minor faucet drips, single slow fixture drain, fixture replacement, planned renovations. We answer the phone live during business hours and have an on-call team for after-hours emergencies.

Quick answers

Frequently asked.

How do I know if I have a plumbing emergency in Middle Tennessee?
True plumbing emergencies are: active water leaks that won't stop with the fixture shut off, complete loss of water to the house, no hot water in cold weather, sewer back-up into living spaces, and suspected gas leaks. For each of these, shut off the relevant supply (water meter, gas at Piedmont's exterior valve, or unit shutoff) and call us. Everything else can usually wait until next-day scheduling.
Do you offer 24/7 emergency plumbing service in Middle Tennessee?
Yes — we have an emergency service team for active leaks, no-water situations, and major back-ups outside business hours. Call our main number and follow the prompts; the on-call plumber will return your call quickly. Standard service calls during regular business hours (Mon–Fri 8 AM–4:30 PM) are scheduled normally.
How much does a plumbing service call cost in Middle Tennessee?
Our standard residential service call is $145, applied to the cost of the repair if you proceed. Once we diagnose the issue, we provide a flat-price repair quote — no per-hour open meter. Major projects (sewer line replacement, water heater install, repipe) are quoted flat after a site visit.
