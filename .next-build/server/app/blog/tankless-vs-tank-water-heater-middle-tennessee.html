Tankless vs. Tank Water Heater in Middle Tennessee: Which Should You Buy?

Tankless water heaters make sense for some Middle Tennessee homes and don't for others. Here's the actual math — payback, service life, hard-water impact, and how to decide.

AK

Aaron Kelly

Licensed Master Plumber, Honest Plumbing

What each technology actually does

A tank water heater stores 30–80 gallons of pre-heated water and fires its burner (gas) or elements (electric) to keep the tank at temperature. When you draw hot water, you're using stored water and the heater fires to replace it. A tankless water heater stores nothing — it fires only when there's flow demand and heats water on-demand as it passes through the unit. Tankless units have higher BTU output (180,000–199,000 BTU is common) because they have to heat water in real-time rather than over a recovery period. Both work; both are appropriate for different situations.

Cost difference in the Middle Tennessee

Installed cost comparison in Middle Tennessee (2026 pricing): standard 40–50 gallon gas tank water heater, $1,400–$2,800 fully installed. Tankless gas water heater conversion, $3,500–$6,500 fully installed. The conversion cost spread is driven mostly by two factors: whether the existing gas line needs upsizing (most Middle Tennessee homes built before about 2010 have a half-inch gas line that won't feed a high-BTU tankless, so we run a three-quarter or one-inch line, adding $400–$1,200), and whether new concentric venting needs to run through an exterior wall ($300–$800 depending on routing). For homes where the gas line is already sized and venting is straightforward, tankless conversion can land closer to $3,500. For homes where both need work, $5,500–$6,500 is realistic.

Operating cost: where the savings actually come from

A typical Middle Tennessee household using a 50-gallon gas tank water heater pays $32–$45/month in natural gas attributable to water heating. A natural-gas tankless serving the same household pays $24–$36/month. Annual savings: roughly $80–$140. That's the real number — not the inflated "save 40% on your water heating bill!" marketing numbers some manufacturers publish. Electric tankless savings vs. electric tank are smaller (electric tanks are already 95%+ efficient at the unit; the savings come from no standby loss). For most Middle Tennessee homes, the operating-cost savings are real but modest — meaningful over a 15–20 year horizon, less compelling over a 5-year horizon.

Where Middle Tennessee's hard water changes the calculation

Middle Tennessee water averages around 70–90 ppm hardness — moderately hard. That's enough to scale up a tankless heat exchanger over time, and a tankless unit that doesn't get descaled annually loses efficiency, then starts throwing error codes, and eventually needs a heat exchanger replacement ($700–$1,200) at year 8–12 instead of year 18–22. Annual descaling ($185 in our service) is non-negotiable to hit the full service-life advantage that tankless promises. Some homeowners install a softener or scale-inhibitor specifically for the tankless ($800–$1,800 installed) as additional protection — this is worth the cost in many cases, especially in the harder-water pockets of the metro.

Service life: where tankless really wins

Standard tank water heaters in Middle Tennessee last 10–13 years on average, sometimes 14–15 with aggressive annual maintenance. Tankless units last 18–22 years with annual descaling, sometimes 25+ with a softener installed. Over a 30-year home ownership horizon, you'd typically buy two or three tanks vs. one or two tankless units. The lifetime cost math at 30 years comes out roughly comparable — tankless is more expensive per unit but you buy fewer of them — with tankless winning when you factor in operating cost savings. The break-even point is usually around year 8–10 of ownership.

When tank is the right choice

Tank water heaters are the better choice when: you're a short-term homeowner (under 5–8 years), your current tank is appropriately sized and you don't run out of hot water, your gas line and venting would require significant rework for a tankless, or you want the lowest-upfront-cost replacement. There's nothing wrong with installing a quality tank water heater in 2026 — millions of perfectly good Middle Tennessee homes run on them. We install Bradford White, AO Smith, and State Industries tanks every day, and they're a perfectly reasonable choice for most homes.

When tankless is the right choice

Tankless makes sense when: you'll be in the home 10+ years (you'll see the payback), you currently run out of hot water during peak use (a tankless gives essentially unlimited hot water at the GPM rating), you want to recover the tank's floor space, your gas line is already adequately sized (or sizing is included cleanly in the conversion quote), or you're planning major bathroom remodels with soaking tubs or high-flow showers that would tax a tank's capacity. Most of our Brentwood, Nolensville, Smyrna, and Brentwood tankless installs are for homeowners hitting one or more of these criteria.

What we'd install in our own home in Middle Tennessee

Honest answer from Aaron Kelly: in my own home (1940s Murfreesboro bungalow, two adults, no kids at home), a Bradford White 50-gallon gas tank water heater. Reason: the house has adequate hot water capacity at the existing tank size, gas line is half-inch and not worth upsizing for the marginal savings, and at our usage pattern the payback math on tankless is closer to 12 years than 6. In a different home — say, a five-bedroom Brentwood house with two teenagers and a soaking tub — I'd install a Rinnai RU199 tankless without hesitation. Different homes, different right answers.

Quick answers

Frequently asked.

How much does it cost to convert from tank to tankless water heater in Middle Tennessee?
Tankless conversions in the Middle Tennessee: $3,500–$6,500 fully installed. The spread is driven by gas line upsizing (most older Middle Tennessee homes need it) and venting work. We provide flat-price quotes after the in-home assessment, with the full scope itemized — no surprise mid-job upcharges.
Will a tankless water heater save me money in Middle Tennessee?
Operating cost savings on a natural-gas tankless vs. tank in Middle Tennessee are about $80–$140/year. Payback on the upfront cost difference typically lands at year 8–12 of ownership, depending on usage pattern and current gas rates. For homeowners who stay 10+ years, tankless usually wins on lifetime cost. For shorter-term homeowners, tank is more economical.
How long does a tankless water heater last in Middle Tennessee?
18–22 years with annual descaling (required for Middle Tennessee's moderately hard water). 25+ years if a water softener is also installed. Without annual descaling, expect 8–12 years before a heat exchanger failure — significantly less than the unit's design life.
Do I need a bigger gas line for a tankless water heater in Middle Tennessee?
Almost certainly yes if your home was built before 2010. A typical half-inch existing gas line won't feed the BTU demand of a tankless. We run a three-quarter or one-inch line from the meter as part of the conversion, included in the quoted price. Newer Brentwood and Franklin homes often already have an adequately sized gas line and don't need the upgrade.
